# Versioning the BTABoK

I have been thinking a lot about what it means for a body of knowledge to be alive. Not alive in the sense of breathing, eating, or sleeping, but alive in the sense of growing, maturing, and sometimes stumbling. The BTABoK is not a static artifact. It is not a sacred text carved into marble that we must never question. It is a working body of knowledge, created by and for architects, and like us it must evolve if it is to remain relevant.

And yet, evolution without accountability is chaos. If we change things without record, if we rewrite ideas without trace, then we are not practicing a profession — we are just scribbling on napkins. Professions are built on transparency, responsibility, and the ability to cite what was known, when it was known, and why it changed. That’s why versioning the BTABoK is not a small technicality. It is part of our **contract with society** — a promise that this body of knowledge will be traceable, reliable, and trustworthy.

---

## What Counts as a Version?

In our work, not every change is equal. Sometimes we overhaul an entire practice, rethink a model, or introduce an entirely new pillar of architecture. Other times we correct a typo or swap out an outdated reference. Both matter, but they do not matter in the same way. To handle this, the BTABoK will recognize three levels of versioning.

1. **Major Versions (BTABoK X.0)**  
   These are the tectonic shifts. A new structure for the knowledge base, a new set of professional pillars, a redefinition of what it means to be an architect. Major versions are rare and deliberate. They require deep community review and formal approval. Think of them as new editions of a book.

2. **Minor Versions (BTABoK X.Y)**  
   These capture incremental growth: new canvases, added competencies, reorganized engagement models. They represent the steady improvement of the BTABoK and are released more frequently than major versions. A minor version says: “we are better than last year, but we are still the same body.”

3. **Document Versions and Revisions (Article vA.B / rN)**  
   Every article in the BTABoK has its own heartbeat. An article may evolve faster than the book as a whole, and that’s okay.
   
   - **Version (vA.B)** is for significant changes to structure or framing — the kind of edits that make you rethink how you apply the content.
   
   - **Revision (rN)** is for smaller updates: examples, diagrams, references, or clarifications.

This gives us a hierarchy like: **BTABoK 3.1 → Architecture Lifecycle v2.3r4.** Anyone, anywhere, can cite exactly what they read.

---

## The Flow of Change

So how does change actually happen? Not by fiat. Not by one person with a red pen and an ego. Change in the BTABoK must follow a process that reflects how architects make decisions.

1. **Proposal** – Anyone can propose a change, large or small. Think of it like an RFC (Request for Comment).

2. **Discussion** – Proposals are debated, refined, challenged, and sometimes torn apart in comments. Architects love to argue, and this is where the arguing belongs.

3. **Approval** – Small revisions can be approved by the editorial committee. Larger ones — especially those that shift the profession — require board or community ratification.

4. **Publication** – Approved changes are published with a clear version tag. Older versions remain visible and citable. Nothing disappears into the void.

This process is not bureaucracy. It is governance. It is what separates a body of knowledge from a blog post. It ensures that what we put into the BTABoK carries weight.

---

## Why Versioning is a Professional Obligation

Doctors don’t just say, “Well, we used to think leeches were a good idea, but now we don’t.” They have journals, editions, references, and case law. Engineers don’t build bridges based on the rumor of a new load-bearing formula. They rely on published, versioned standards.

If we want architecture to be a **Big P Profession**, then we too must hold ourselves to this level of rigor. A versioning system is not about control. It is about trust. It is about showing the world that what we know, we know together, and what we change, we change transparently.

Versioning allows:

- Researchers to track how thinking evolves.

- Practitioners to reference stable points in time.

- Educators to align curricula with official knowledge.

- Communities to see their input acknowledged in history.

And perhaps most importantly, it prevents us from rewriting our own story. It forces us to remember what we once believed — even when we no longer agree with it.

---

## A Living System

Of course, versioning is not just a philosophy. It needs a system behind it. The BTABoK will use open tools and open practices:

- Articles stored in Git repositories with metadata for version and revision.

- Tags for major and minor releases of the whole body.

- Discussion threads tied to proposals, so history is never lost.

- A simple front-end that allows anyone to switch between “current,” “previous,” and “diff” views.

It will not be perfect. No system is. But it will be ours, and it will grow with us. Architects love to improve things, and this system will be no exception.

---

## Final Thought

If we are serious about architecture as a profession, then we must be serious about accountability. Versioning is not an afterthought. It is the skeleton that holds the BTABoK together as it grows. Without it, we are just telling stories. With it, we are writing history.

So the next time someone asks, “What does the BTABoK say about lifecycles?” we won’t just answer with an opinion or a half-remembered paragraph. We will be able to point to **exactly the version, revision, and context** in which that knowledge was written. And that — more than any single model or canvas — is what makes a body of knowledge truly professional.
